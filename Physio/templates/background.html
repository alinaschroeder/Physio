Relevant Physiology

The basal ganglia are a group of nuclei that sit at the base of the forebrain. The basal ganglia are associated with many functions including voluntary motor control, eye movement and cognitive and emotional functions. “Basal ganglia” is a term that encompases the striatum, the globus pallidus, the substantia nigra, and the subthalamic nucleus [1]. Functions of the basal ganglia:

The basal ganglia is necessary to enable practiced motor acts and in gating the initiation of voluntary movements. They are also involved in selecting and enabling cognitive and emotional programs stored in cortical areas. It seems that the basal ganglia, specifically the striatum, is also involved in certain types of learning [2].

The striatum is the main recipient of afferents to the basal ganglia. It is the largest component of the basal ganglia and is made up of the caudate nucleus and the putamen. This section receives input from other sections of the brain but can only transmit information to other parts within the basal ganglia.

The globus pallidus and the substantia nigra are the major output structures within the basal ganglia. These structures make GABAergic inhibitory connections on their targets. The subthalamic nucleus receives information from the cerebral cortex and sends information to the globus pallidus. It is part of the purely excitatory pathway within the basal ganglia [2]. It makes glutaminergic, excitatory connections on both segments of the globus pallidus and the substantia nigra pars reticula [2].The substantia nigra has two functions: inhibiting the function of the thalamus and production of dopamine.

Pathways in the basal ganglia:

There are 2 pathways within the basal ganglia, the direct pathway and the indirect pathway. The direct pathway excites thalamic neurons, which then excites cortical neurons. The indirect pathway has the opposite effect and inhibits the thalamic neurons. In a normally functioning brain, there is a balance between these 2 pathways.

The direct pathway is when cells in the striatum make inhibitory connections with cells in the internal part of the globus pallidus [2]. The cells in the globus pallidus then make inhibitory connections on the cells in the thalamus. Firing of the globus pallidus neurons inhibits the thalamus, but the inhibition of the neurons causes the thalamic neurons to be released from inhibition. Now the thalamic neurons are free to fire and excite the cortex.

The indirect pathway is when different cells in the striatum make inhibitory connections with the external part of the globus pallidus. Those cells then make inhibitory connections with cells in the subthalamic nucleus. Then those cells make excitatory connections to cells on the internal part of the globus pallidus. Like previously said, firing of these neurons will inhibit the thalamus, so in the indirect pathway a net inhibition on the motor cortex is produced [2].
